\subsection{Mechanistic Formalization}
\label{app:theory-bridge}
This appendix gives the assumptions and proofs for the three compact propositions stated in Setup.
The goal is not a general theorem about deep networks under shift.
It is to formalize the selector mismatch created by suppressive weighting, explain why the validation-loss guardrail helps, and give a principled bridge from the empirical time courses to the transient-versus-persistent split.

\paragraph{Proposition 1 (Local lure under suppressive weighting).}
Assume a suppressive objective whose high-loss slope ratio is $\alpha \in (0,1)$.
Let $A_c>0$ denote core improvement mass and $A_t>0$ denote tail damage mass over a short interval.
For local loss tradeoffs in which the core improvement is on examples below the clipping threshold and the tail damage is on examples above it, there is a non-empty region in which the suppressive proxy improves while standard risk worsens, namely $\alpha A_t < A_c < A_t$.

\emph{Proof.}
For soft clipping with threshold $\tau$ and slope $\alpha \in (0,1]$,
\[
\tilde{\ell} = \ell - (1-\alpha)(\ell-\tau)_+,
\]
so clipping removes upper-tail loss mass directly at the per-example level.
When $\tau=\mathrm{VaR}_q(\ell)$, the same monotonicity gives a compressed proxy tail
\[
\mathrm{CVaR}_q(\tilde{\ell}) = \tau + \alpha\big(\mathrm{CVaR}_q(\ell)-\tau\big),
\]
which is why the proxy can look improved even when the underlying standard-loss tail has not.

\paragraph{Local lure condition.}
Let $A_c>0$ denote core improvement mass and $A_t>0$ denote tail damage mass over a short interval.
The short-interval approximation assumes that the core improvement mass lies below the clipping threshold, where the proxy slope is $1$, and the tail damage mass lies above it, where the proxy slope is $\alpha$.
Then standard risk and suppressive proxy move as
\[
\Delta R \approx -A_c + A_t,
\qquad
\Delta P \approx -A_c + \alpha A_t.
\]
Whenever
\[
\alpha A_t < A_c < A_t,
\]
the proxy still improves while the standard risk worsens.
So the local lure region is non-empty whenever $\alpha<1$.
It widens as $\alpha$ decreases, which is why stronger suppression creates more room for proxy-only selection to move toward less reliable checkpoints.
\hfill$\square$

\paragraph{Proposition 2 (Baseline-referenced guardrail bound).}
Assume the guardrail enforces
\[
L_{\mathrm{val}}(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{guard}}) \le \rho L_{\mathrm{val}}(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{base}})
\]
and that validation and deployment losses satisfy
\[
\sup_t \left|L_{\mathrm{test}}(\theta_t)-L_{\mathrm{val}}(\theta_t)\right| \le \varepsilon
\]
over the candidate suppressive checkpoints and also for the baseline checkpoint $\hat\theta_{\mathrm{base}}$.
Then
\[
L_{\mathrm{test}}(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{guard}})
\le
\rho\,L_{\mathrm{test}}(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{base}})
+
(\rho+1)\varepsilon.
\]

\emph{Proof.}
By the uniform closeness assumption,
\[
L_{\mathrm{test}}(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{guard}})
\le
L_{\mathrm{val}}(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{guard}})+\varepsilon
\le
\rho\,L_{\mathrm{val}}(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{base}})+\varepsilon.
\]
Applying the same bound to the baseline checkpoint gives
\[
L_{\mathrm{val}}(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{base}})
\le
L_{\mathrm{test}}(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{base}})+\varepsilon,
\]
so
\[
L_{\mathrm{test}}(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{guard}})
\le
\rho\,L_{\mathrm{test}}(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{base}})
+
(\rho+1)\varepsilon.
\]
This does not make the guardrail optimal, but it does show why the rule is not arbitrary: the budget caps deployment loss relative to baseline up to validation-to-test mismatch, while proxy-only selection has no analogous guarantee without assuming that the suppressive proxy stays aligned with standard reliability metrics.
\hfill$\square$

\paragraph{Proposition 3 (Transient versus persistent in a two-phase toy).}
Let $H_A\ge 0$ denote the excess standard-loss harm accumulated during an early suppressive phase, and let $H_B\ge 0$ denote the later recovery.
Then the net hazard is persistent when $H_A > H_B$ and transient when $H_B \ge H_A$.

\emph{Proof.}
By construction, the two-phase toy decomposes the total standard-loss effect into an early excess-harm term and a later recovery term.
The sign of the net effect is therefore the sign of $H_A-H_B$.
If $H_A > H_B$, the run remains worse than baseline at the matched horizon; if $H_B \ge H_A$, the excess harm has been offset and the matched-horizon hazard disappears.
\hfill$\square$

The two fixed teacher-difficulty banks are produced once per stack by \texttt{src/scripts/build\_conf\_eval\_banks.py}.

For finetuning, the selected-checkpoint bankwise direction remains at P95, P97, and P99 as well: the harmful mean tail direction holds on both banks, with slightly weaker decoupling fractions at P95 on both banks and P97 on bank B.

\subsection{ACSIncome Huber Dose-Response}
\label{app:acsincome-huber}
On the same ACSIncome regression stack, Huber loss at the P95 residual threshold provides a milder suppressive objective ($R_w = 0.53$, vs.\ $0.41$ for winsorization).
The selection hazard is weaker and shifts toward the tail channel: proxy-only selection worsens raw held-out MSE on $3/10$ seeds and tail MSE on $9/10$ seeds.
The mean $\Delta$\,tail MSE is $+0.051$ with $95\%$ CI $[+0.028, +0.073]$ (excludes zero), while the mean $\Delta$\,held-out MSE is slightly negative at $-0.0031$ with $95\%$ CI $[-0.0053, -0.0008]$.
This is consistent with Proposition~1's dose-response picture: Huber preserves a weaker but non-zero tail signal, and the ordering $R_w^{\text{wins}} = 0.41 < R_w^{\text{huber}} = 0.53$ maps to a much stronger winsorization hazard.

\subsection{Hard-Bootstrap Corroboration}
Table~\ref{tab:bootstrap-camelyon-support} gives the fuller second-family selector study on the same Camelyon17 ERM stack using hard bootstrapped cross-entropy with $\beta=0.60$, a standard suppressive objective from the noisy-label literature.
The core selector mismatch survives this extension.
Relative to val-loss-only selection inside the same bootstrap runs, proxy-only improves the bootstrap proxy by about $0.010$ but worsens held-out hospital-2 loss by $+0.0109$, teacher-difficulty tail CVaR by $+1.37$, and ECE by $+0.0110$ on average across ten seeds.
The selected checkpoints remain suppressive ($R_w \approx 0.567$, early $R_w \approx 0.605$).

The fixed $1.25\times$ guardrail is effectively slack on this family, while the within-run oracle-loss selector lands much earlier (mean epoch $6.4$ versus $15.2$ for proxy-only) and is lower on held-out loss, teacher-difficulty tail CVaR, and ECE.
Unlike the main P95 soft-clipping anchor, this bootstrap corroboration is not a stronger fixed-horizon pathology: relative to fixed epoch 30, the selected bootstrap checkpoint is actually better on proxy, held-out loss, teacher-difficulty tail CVaR, and ECE.
So we use it as supporting evidence against the ``only soft clipping'' objection rather than as a replacement for the main hazard anchors.
